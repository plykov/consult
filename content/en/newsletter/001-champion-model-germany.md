title: Your champion isn't your buyer — selling to the German decision unit

summary: Why the single-champion playbook that built your home-market pipeline quietly kills your German deals, and what to map instead.
---

The most expensive assumption in European expansion is that a deal has *a* buyer.

In the US motion most technical companies grow up with, one enthusiastic champion with budget authority can carry a deal end-to-end. Your entire playbook is tuned to find, arm and accelerate that one person.

In Germany, that person usually exists — and usually cannot buy anything alone.

**The decision unit is the real customer.** A mid-size German enterprise deal typically involves engineering, IT security, legal, purchasing, and — for anything touching employee data or workflows — the works council (*Betriebsrat*). Each of these is not an "influencer" to be name-dropped in your CRM. Each one can stop the deal, and each expects to be engaged in its own language, with its own proof.

**Three practical moves:**

1. **Map the unit before you accelerate.** In discovery, ask directly: "Who besides you needs to say yes, and what does each of them need to see?" German buyers answer this question happily. Most vendors never ask it.
2. **Build stakeholder-specific proof.** A security whitepaper for the CISO, a data-processing agreement draft for legal, a reference call for purchasing. One generic deck serves nobody.
3. **Treat the works council as a stakeholder, not an obstacle.** If your product touches employee workflows, address co-determination early and factually. Vendors who handle this well are rare — and remembered.

The champion still matters. But their job changes: from buyer to guide through the unit. Arm them for that job, and your German cycle times start looking like your home-market ones.
